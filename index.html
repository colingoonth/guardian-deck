The gap in the market

Every existing solution checks the number.
None of them listen to the caller.

What exists today

T-Mobile Scam Shield, RoboKiller, Truecaller, Nomorobo — they all do the same thing: check if the phone number is in a database.

A scammer spoofs a new number and every single one of them lets the call through. They can't catch what they can't see.

No real-time analysis. No AI. No understanding of what the caller actually says.

What's missing

A system that doesn't just check who's calling — but listens to what they're saying.

Something that intercepts the call, asks the caller to identify themselves, and uses AI to determine if it's a scam in real time — before the phone ever rings.

The fundamental problem: databases are reactive. Scammers are proactive.

Walkthrough

What happens when an unknown number calls

1
Call arrives at VoIP.ms
The caller dials grandma's number. VoIP.ms receives the SIP connection and forwards it to our Asterisk PBX — grandma's phone never rings yet.
2
Blacklist check — 64K known scam numbers
Asterisk runs the AGI script. First check: is this number in our blacklist? Built from FTC Do Not Call complaints and FCC Unwanted Call reports. If yes —
Instant hangup
3
Whitelist check — 197K trusted institutions
Hospitals, police departments, fire stations, EMS, schools, pharmacies. Sourced from 13 US government datasets (CMS, HIFLD, SSA, NCES). If the number matches —
Check STIR/SHAKEN [V] verification
4
Spoof detection — carrier cryptographic verification
If the number claims to be a trusted institution, we verify the carrier signed it with STIR/SHAKEN A-level attestation. VoIP.ms prepends [V] to verified calls. No [V]? Possible spoof — route to AI screening.
5
AI screening — "Who's calling and why?"
For truly unknown numbers: the IVR answers, asks the caller to identify themselves, records their response. Whisper transcribes it, DistilBERT classifies it as safe or scam — 2ms inference, zero cloud cost.
